Convex Seascape Survey: Quantifying and understanding carbon stores in the coastal ocean floor to harness the power of the sea in the fight against climate change.

Blue Marine Foundation (
Non-governmental organization (NGO)

    Description
    Description

    Blue Marine Foundation, the University of Exeter and Convex Insurance Group have partnered on an ambitious five-year global research programme to quantify and understand the blue carbon stored in the coastal ocean floor. Working in collaboration with experts from around the world, we will fill knowledge gaps on how the ocean and climate are connected, to inform effective action against the climate crisis.

    Our research programme looks at several crucial topics. Firstly, we're studying the origins of carbon in the world's continental shelves and how it has accumulated and altered over time, as well as identifying where the biggest carbon stores are found and putting them into context in the global carbon cycle. We're also studying the impact of human influences on seascape carbon by mapping the historical spread of human disturbance and overlaying these maps with the distribution of carbon in the seascape. Concurrently, we're measuring the consequences of bottom disturbance to understand the impact of contemporary disturbance on blue carbon and it's vulnerability to loss and re-lease into the ocean and atmosphere. Finally we are measuring how long it takes for carbon uptake to recover when the seabed is protected, investigating the role of marine life and biodiversity on these processes, and quantifying the complementary benefits to wildlife and people.

    The coastal seas of the world’s continental shelves are the ocean’s biggest carbon store and a major driver of carbon sequestration. We hope that by identifying carbon stores, and making quantitative links between human pressures on the seabed and their impact on carbon release, as well as the role of protection, we can identify potential marine management options to slow climate change. With careful management, coastal seas could provide an estimated 6 percent of the carbon drawdown needed to slow the rise in global temperature to 1.5 degrees. Our survey results will inform decisions on ocean use and raise the profile of the ocean as a nature-based solution to climate change.
