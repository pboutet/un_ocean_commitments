Rights of the Galapagos and the Hermandad Marine Reserve and life within it to a pollution free future: accelerating solutions

    Description
    Description

    As a collective, our overarching goal is to take a holistic, solutions-oriented approach to protecting the rights of the species and local communities in the Galapagos Marine Reserve to exist in a healthy, pollution-free environment. Our objectives are: (i) to reduce the risks to wildlife and local livelihoods, (ii) eliminate illegal drifting Fish Aggregating Devices (FADs) in the marine reserve, (iii) work towards a systemic shift towards a regenerative island economy focusing on regenerative tourism and a circular economy, and (iv) continue to cultivate and engage the international Pacific Plastics: Science to Solutions network to share expertise, fuel solution co-design and scaling, forge regional collaborations in the Eastern Pacific and support early career ocean professionals.

    Our implementation methodologies include a range of science, policy, education and technology approaches working from grassroots to government. For example, we are working with a range of local to international stakeholders to develop scientifically sound and community-informed monitoring protocols (including those adapted for citizen science) and systems that Park Rangers can easily use. Further, we are embracing new technological solutions, capacity strengthening opportunities and in-country legal expertise to inform and co-design community-led approaches to protected area management and enforcement. Targeted communications, outreach and briefings designed for key audiences and government stakeholders are integral to our work, promoting awareness of pollution impacts in Galapagos and tangible solutions for how to address these.

    Good governance is ensured with clear partnership agreements/MOUs with partners and clear communication prioritised internally and externally. GCT has 30 years' experience of managing grants to a wide spectrum of partners and we continue to refine our due diligence procedures to ensure we are compliant with all legal requirements etc. From founding and managing the Pacific Plastics Science to Solutions (PPSS) network since 2021, and now joining the Tangaroa Alliance, we will scale and exchange our knowledge and solutions on a global scale. The Tangaroa Alliance is a Pacific Island collective established to jointly tackle the multiple planetary crisis affecting the region: climate change, plastic pollution, and biodiversity loss. Rooted in the leadership of Indigenous and local communities and governments, this initiative fosters an inter-island collaboration model with a focus on circular economy and ocean restoration and is highly complementary to the PPSS approach (please see the Tangaroa Alliance commitment).

    Deliverables & Timeline
    31 December, 2030
    Support the implementation of robust annual monitoring and clean up activities under the Strategic Plan for the management of oceanic plastic pollution to 2030, led by the Galapagos National Park Authority in collaboration with supporters.
    31 December, 2027
    Reduce threats in the Galapagos and Hermandad Marine Reserves from drifting FADs and plastic waste dumped at sea through science- and community-informed management actions and policy solutions generated across 2025-2027.
    31 December, 2030
    Pilot solutions to avoid single-use plastics (SUPs) in Galapagos, building a regenerative, circular economy supported through the adoption of reformed policies and scaling of community innovations that reduce consumption and improve management of SUPs.
    31 December, 2030
    The PPSS network aims to scale solutions that tackle ocean plastic waste in the Pacific, whilst supporting Early Career Ocean Professionals. It will provide an interface for knowledge exchange between science, community and policy stakeholders.

    Other beneficiaries
    The unique biodiversity protected by the Galapagos Marine Reserve is also protected from the incoming threat of oceanic pollution. Galapagos residents (community of c.30,000 people) including local users of the Galapagos Marine Reserve such as artisanal fishers (c. 1,000), park rangers, naturalist guides (c. 400) and tourism operators. Alternative livelihoods provide opportunities for Galapagos communities. Galapagos tourists (c. 350,000). Other MPAs in the Eastern Pacific benefit from possible spillover effects, knowledge and solution sharing and complimentary tools/ approaches. Early career ocean professionals in Galapagos and the international research community will benefit from professional development opportunities.
