COSMARINDO - Coral Spawning Monitoring and Awareness for Restoration Network Indonesia

    Description
    Description

    The main purpose of this project is to grow a network of local researchers and coral reef practitioners to improve the understanding of coral spawning patterns as well as promote non-invasive reef restoration methods. This network will contribute data to an open-access database, enhancing the understanding of coral spawning patterns across Indonesia's vast reef systems.
    We aim to develop an Indonesian-wide student network for monitoring coral spawning through training six universities in Indonesia with a combination of online and onsite workshops, during which students learn coral identification, how to monitor coral spawning in a non-invasive way, and get trained in sustainable restoration techniques, such as larval propagation. Important learning components will also be how to follow a scientific framework of decision-making (e.g. method and site selection), project design (including environmental and ethical principles) and monitoring of failure and success (e.g. short and long-term monitoring of restoration sites). Through the bilingual network, students will receive access to state-of-the-art techniques. The final workshop series will focus on monitoring coral settlement and growth and emphasize the importance of long-term follow-up after the restoration effort. By focusing on capacity building and education, the project seeks to create a sustainable future for coral restoration in Indonesia, with an emphasis on scientifically informed practices.
    Another reason why larval propagation has not been used as a restoration tool in Indonesia is missing information on spawning patterns. We, therefore, are creating a centralized, mobile-friendly, and freely accessible online platform where participants can add observations of spawning from all over Indonesia. This database will be focused on ease of use and will allow more specific and cost-efficient monitoring in the future. It will also motivate the private sector to contribute. Data will later be fed to the wider coral spawning database Indo-Pacific (Baird et al. 2021).
    In addition, we are comparing coral spawning patterns between shallow and mesophotic reefs by developing a camera-based monitoring system that can be deployed for 3-4 weeks at up to 80 m depth. Understanding coral spawning times in shallow and mesophotic reefs not only supports the implementation of larval propagation, but also helps with other conservation and management decisions, and is key for future climate change scenarios where interventions after mass bleaching events will likely become necessary to maintain coral diversity. Furthermore, we will also give an introduction to safe deep diving practice to students for future technical diving training and enable Indonesian researchers to monitor mesophotic reefs effectively.
    An important goal of COSMARINDO is to achieve sustainability in workflows and funding within the participating university teams and organizations. To achieve independence and sustainability in the working groups at the participating universities, we will work out a co-financing network by partnering university teams with nearby dive operators and Conservation Organizations / NGOs. The level of international collaboration in the network, and the opportunities for international publications resulting from monitoring, will be excellent incentives for the faculties to continue supporting COSMARINDO.

    Other beneficiaries
    Six universities in Indonesia (Banda Neira University, Pattimura University, Hasanuddin University, Mataram University, Sam Ratulangi University, and Udayana University) will benefit from a training program on coral identification and coral spawning monitoring. They will also gain acces to network that connect them with international scientists in their field. In addition, the outcomes of COSMARINDO could support the work of Indonesian researchers, local NGOs, and marine tourism stakeholders. For example, the coral spawning and monitoring application that we are going to develop would help researchers and NGOs to better understand the productive behavior of corals, which would then help them in planning the dive activity when they start monitoring coral spawning and restoring coral reefs via larval propagation method. Dive centers can also create an engaging experience with guests, such as "Coral Spawning Night Dive". This will be an educational experience for the guests, who will have the opportunity to participate as citizen scientists in the project and know more about coral reef ecosystem biology and conservation.
