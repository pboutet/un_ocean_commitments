Global Ocean Shield: Geospatial Resilience Against Climate Disasters

    Description
    Description
    The Global Ocean Shield initiative, led by the World Disaster Center (WDC), aims to enhance ocean and coastal resilience against climate-driven disasters such as tsunamis, hurricanes, and flooding, which threaten marine ecosystems and exacerbate pollution. Our objectives are to protect marine and coastal ecosystems (SDG 14.2), reduce disaster-related marine pollution (SDG 14.1), and advance scientific knowledge and technology transfer (SDG 14.a) for vulnerable coastal communities, particularly in Small Island Developing States (SIDS). WDC leverages its Michael platform, integrating satellite imagery, seismic sensors, and social media analytics for real-time disaster monitoring. Partnering with Esri, we utilize ArcGIS for geospatial mapping to assess coastal vulnerabilities and predict disaster impacts. Google’s cloud computing and AI enhance data processing, enabling rapid identification of marine pollution risks (e.g., debris from floods or oil spills). JBA Consulting contributes flood risk modeling to strengthen coastal ecosystem management, while UNOCHA coordinates humanitarian responses to ensure timely aid delivery to affected communities. UNDRR aligns our efforts with the Sendai Framework, promoting disaster risk reduction strategies that safeguard marine biodiversity. Implementation involves deploying geospatial tools to monitor ocean basins globally, with a focus on high-risk areas like the Indian Ocean and Pacific. We develop early warning systems for tsunamis and storm surges, map pollution hotspots post-disaster, and provide training to local stakeholders in SIDS for technology adoption. Data-sharing platforms, supported by Google and Esri, ensure accessibility for governments and communities, fostering collaborative resilience-building. Pilot projects in select coastal regions will test predictive models, integrating real-time data with JBA’s flood risk assessments to protect mangroves and wetlands as natural barriers. Follow-up mechanisms include quarterly progress reports, shared via UNOCHA’s humanitarian networks, and annual evaluations using key performance indicators (e.g., reduced disaster response times, decreased pollution incidents). WDC will host workshops with UNDRR to disseminate findings and refine methodologies. Governance is managed by a steering committee comprising WDC, Esri, Google, JBA Consulting, UNOCHA, and UNDRR, ensuring strategic alignment and resource allocation. Local community representatives from SIDS will participate to ensure inclusive decision-making. This initiative strengthens global ocean resilience by combining cutting-edge geospatial technology with international cooperation, delivering actionable insights to protect marine ecosystems and support sustainable development.
    Partners

    World Disaster Center (Non-Governmental Organization): Lead entity, driving geospatial monitoring and disaster response using the Michael platform for ocean-related disasters (e.g., tsunamis, hurricanes, coastal flooding). Esri (Private Sector): Provides GIS technology and ArcGIS platform for geospatial data analysis, enhancing WDC’s disaster monitoring capabilities. Google (Private Sector): Likely contributes cloud computing, mapping tools (e.g., Google Maps/Earth), or AI for real-time disaster data processing. JBA Consulting (Private Sector): Offers expertise in flood risk management and environmental consulting, supporting resilience against ocean-related hazards like coastal flooding. United Nations Office for the Coordination of Humanitarian Affairs (UNOCHA) (Intergovernmental Organization): Facilitates humanitarian response coordination, integrating WDC’s geospatial data for disaster-affected coastal communities. United Nations Office for Disaster Risk Reduction (UNDRR) (Intergovernmental Organization): Supports disaster risk reduction frameworks, aligning with WDC’s monitoring for ocean resilience under the Sendai Framework.

    End poverty in all its forms everywhere

    1.1

    By 2030, eradicate extreme poverty for all people everywhere, currently measured as people living on less than $1.25 a day

    1.1.1

    Proportion of the population living below the international poverty line by sex, age, employment status and geographical location (urban/rural)

    1.2

    By 2030, reduce at least by half the proportion of men, women and children of all ages living in poverty in all its dimensions according to national definitions

    1.2.1

    Proportion of population living below the national poverty line, by sex and age

    1.2.2

    Proportion of men, women and children of all ages living in poverty in all its dimensions according to national definitions

    1.3

    Implement nationally appropriate social protection systems and measures for all, including floors, and by 2030 achieve substantial coverage of the poor and the vulnerable

    1.3.1

    Proportion of population covered by social protection floors/systems, by sex, distinguishing children, unemployed persons, older persons, persons with disabilities, pregnant women, newborns, work-injury victims and the poor and the vulnerable

    1.4

    By 2030, ensure that all men and women, in particular the poor and the vulnerable, have equal rights to economic resources, as well as access to basic services, ownership and control over land and other forms of property, inheritance, natural resources, appropriate new technology and financial services, including microfinance

    1.4.1

    Proportion of population living in households with access to basic services

    1.4.2

    Proportion of total adult population with secure tenure rights to land, (a) with legally recognized documentation, and (b) who perceive their rights to land as secure, by sex and by type of tenure

    1.5

    By 2030, build the resilience of the poor and those in vulnerable situations and reduce their exposure and vulnerability to climate-related extreme events and other economic, social and environmental shocks and disasters

    1.5.1

    Number of deaths, missing persons and directly affected persons attributed to disasters per 100,000 population

    1.5.2

    Direct economic loss attributed to disasters in relation to global gross domestic product (GDP)

    1.5.3

    Number of countries that adopt and implement national disaster risk reduction strategies in line with the Sendai Framework for Disaster Risk Reduction 2015-2030

    1.5.4

    Proportion of local governments that adopt and implement local disaster risk reduction strategies in line with national disaster risk reduction strategies

    1.a

    Ensure significant mobilization of resources from a variety of sources, including through enhanced development cooperation, in order to provide adequate and predictable means for developing countries, in particular least developed countries, to implement programmes and policies to end poverty in all its dimensions

    Total official development assistance grants that focus on poverty reduction as a share of the recipient country’s gross national income

    1.a.2

    Pro-poor public social spending
